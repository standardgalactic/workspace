What you have here is a remarkably coherent philosophical argument built around a single unifying idea, and it can be summarized as a progressive dismantling of the common notion of “stability” and its replacement with a dynamic, process-based conception of identity .

At the outset, the text contrasts the intuitive, clinical model of diagnosis with more complex domains. In medicine, a broken bone can be identified with mechanical certainty, producing a sense of clarity and comfort. This model assumes that things are either intact or broken, fixed and classifiable. However, once one moves into domains such as psychology, culture, or identity, this model fails. The objects of analysis are no longer static; they are constantly shifting, and thus resist simple classification .

From here, the central conceptual shift is introduced through the contrast between a stone and a flame. A stone represents classical stability: it remains unchanged by resisting interaction. A flame, by contrast, maintains its identity precisely through continuous change. Its material is constantly replaced, yet its form persists. The argument is that most meaningful structures—language, identity, culture—are not “stone-like” but “flame-like.” They are not fixed substances but ongoing processes that must be actively maintained .

This leads to the formal core of the paper: stability is defined as “return under restriction.” A system remains stable not by avoiding disturbance, but by repeatedly returning to a recognizable state despite disturbance. This is framed using the idea of Lyapunov stability, where a system can be perturbed yet does not diverge over time. Stability becomes an active achievement rather than a passive condition .

The mechanism underlying this is expressed as a dual principle: repetition and constraint. Repetition provides continuity by renewing the system over time, while constraints prevent random drift and enforce structure. If repetition occurs without constraints, the system degenerates into distortion, as in the children’s “telephone game.” If constraints exist without repetition, the system becomes rigid and lifeless. Stability emerges only from the balance between these two forces .

This framework is then applied across multiple domains. In language, the text argues that words are not fixed containers of meaning, as dictionaries suggest, but dynamic processes generated by repeated use under structural constraints. The example of Arabic roots shows that seemingly disparate meanings are unified by an underlying dynamic pattern rather than a static definition. Meaning is thus generated, not stored .

In religion and ritual, repetition is shown not to transmit information but to stabilize internal states. Practices such as repeated invocations function as mechanisms that guide attention and enforce convergence toward a stable psychological or spiritual state. The distinction is drawn between declaring certainty and actively producing it through repeated constrained action .

In technology, the printing press is analyzed as a physical instantiation of the same principle. The identity of a text does not reside in its material substrate, which degrades, but in the repeatable process that reconstructs it under strict constraints. This highlights a trade-off between flexibility and fidelity: oral transmission allows adaptation but risks distortion, while printing ensures accuracy but introduces rigidity .

The discussion culminates in a diagnostic framework of failure modes, each corresponding to a breakdown in the balance between repetition and constraint. Systems may drift when constraints are absent, stagnate when repetition ceases, oscillate when corrections overshoot, become blocked under contradictory constraints, or settle into suboptimal equilibria. These patterns apply equally to artificial intelligence, institutions, and individual psychology .

The final metaphor of a touchscreen malfunctioning in the rain illustrates the broader epistemological claim. Systems do not fail because their internal logic is flawed, but because the inputs they receive are noisy, ambiguous, and indistinguishable. Human cognition operates under similar conditions of partial, distorted information. Survival, therefore, does not depend on perfect knowledge but on the capacity to repeatedly return to coherence despite noise .

The concluding insight is that identity itself is not a fixed essence but a dynamic equilibrium. It is the result of repeated actions constrained by environment and structure. Change the constraints or the patterns of repetition, and a new stable identity will emerge. Thus, a person is not a stone fixed in place, but a flame whose stability depends on continuous transformation .
