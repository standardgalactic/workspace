## 1. Nature’s Voronoi: The Leaf as a Mathematical Partition

We often mistake the vascular networks in leaves for optimized transport systems, designed like highways to minimize travel time. However, research into *Pilea peperomioides* (the Chinese money plant) reveals that leaf veins are actually a realized Partition Geometry.

The leaf does not possess a map of its veins. Instead, it positions hydathodes—secretory pores—which act as organizing centers. These centers broadcast biochemical “auxin waves.” Where waves from adjacent hydathodes collide, they create a local concentration maximum—a “skirmish line” that triggers vein formation. This process generates a Voronoi diagram: a mathematical partition where every point within a cell is closer to its own hydathode than to any other.

The quantitative evidence for this is decisive. When tested against a k-d tree (a principled hierarchical alternative), the Voronoi model achieved a Jaccard index of 0.72, while the k-d tree stalled at 0.40.

> “The leaf does not specify the Voronoi diagram... The global geometry emerges from local rules.”

The plant is not solving for transport efficiency; it is solving for spatial balance through the interference of propagating waves. Global order emerges not from a central plan, but from the local equilibrium of an active medium.

## 2. Solid Ground: Why Tissue State is the Message

## 3. Unlocking the Latent Human: Accessibility Over Novelty

The profound implication is that the “human” program is a latent potential within the mammalian manifold, gated only by the timing switches of the constraint geometry.

## 4. Communication Geometry: Routing Through Rotation

Once built, the brain’s geometry continues to evolve, not through anatomical rewiring, but through the orientation of Communication Subspaces. In the CA3–CA1–RSC (retrosplenial cortex) axis, regions communicate by projecting high-dimensional activity onto low-dimensional manifolds.

The “rotation” discovery is computationally load-bearing: the input and output subspaces of CA1 are nearly orthogonal (rotated 90 degrees apart). This geometry is a computational necessity; Partial Canonical Correlation Analysis (pCCA) shows that if these subspaces were fixed or aligned, the brain would suffer from catastrophic interference.

This geometry also governs the duality of memory. During sleep, the intra-hippocampal subspaces (CA3–CA1) are highly plastic, facilitating replay and the encoding of new experiences. Meanwhile, the hippocampal-cortical subspaces (CA1–RSC) remain stable, protecting long-term memories from being overwritten. The brain achieves flexible cognition by “reorienting” its communication geometry, effectively rerouting information through a fixed physical substrate.
